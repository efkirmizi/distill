Project Description

Compressing deep networks for the edge — without breaking them

Modern vision models far exceed the memory and power budgets of drones, IoT sensors and medical-imaging hardware. This project resolves the two failure modes of existing compression by unifying them into one joint optimization.

Tensor decomposition breaks

Factorizing a pre-trained network and fine-tuning it sequentially triggers vanishing gradients and catastrophic accuracy collapse at high compression.

Distillation collapses

Knowledge distillation breaks down under the capacity gap at extreme compression ratios, and becomes ill-defined once a student's layers are tensor-factorized.

Our joint framework

Build the compressed students from scratch and co-train them under one frozen teacher in a single pass — compression and knowledge transfer happen together, not in fragile stages.

Proposed Framework

Three fully automatic stages

Every design choice is derived from the teacher itself — no global compression ratio, no validation search, no hand-tuned coefficients.

Figure 1 — Proposed FrameworkA single joint optimization replaces the sequential pipeline. Both CP and Tucker students are built from scratch and co-trained under one frozen teacher, sharing a single forward pass each step — roughly twice the cost of single-student distillation, not three times.
Stage 1 — Where to supervise

PURSUhInT

K-means clustering on teacher layer representations using 1 − R²CCA distance selects one non-redundant hint point per cluster (K=3 CIFAR, K=4 ImageNet-100). Avoids redundant supervision from semantically similar layers.

Stage 2 — How much to compress

EVBMF Rank Estimation

Reads the teacher's trained weight spectrum and thresholds singular values against the Marchenko–Pastur noise distribution — setting each layer's rank automatically. Non-uniform allocation: early layers ≈59%, deep layers 36–44%.

Stage 3 — Joint training

Dual Co-training

A CP and a Tucker student are co-trained under one shared teacher forward pass, combining cross-entropy, KL-divergence and spatial attention transfer with learned Kendall uncertainty weights — eliminating all manual loss coefficients.

Technical Details

Two complementary decompositions, one teacher

Both students share the same base architecture but factorize every eligible convolution differently, creating a controllable Pareto trade-off between model size and accuracy.

CP Decomposition

4 ops / conv
𝒲 ≈ Σr=1R aᵣ ⊗ bᵣ ⊗ cᵣ ⊗ dᵣ

Replaces each 2D convolution with four separable operations: 1×1 → dw-kH → dw-kW → 1×1. Separable spatial filtering discards all cross-channel interaction outside the pointwise stages — yielding the highest compression ratio.

Params: R(Cin+kH+kW+Cout)Up to 64× compressionUp to 52× FLOPs reduction

Tucker Decomposition

3 ops / conv
𝒲 ≈ 𝒢 ×₁ Uout ×₂ Uin

Replaces each 2D convolution with three operations: 1×1 → kH×kW core → 1×1. The dense core preserves cross-channel correlations across all spatial positions — delivering higher accuracy and lower latency despite more parameters than CP.

Params: RinCin+RinRoutkHkW+RoutCoutSeparate Rout, Rin per layerFaster despite more params

Automatic Loss Balancing — Kendall Uncertainty Weighting

Zero hand-tuned coefficients
ℒ = Σi ∈ {CE, KL, AT} ( e−sᵢ ℒᵢ + sᵢ )

The three losses (cross-entropy, KL-divergence, spatial attention transfer) operate at wildly different numerical scales. Learnable log-variance weights si equalize their gradient contributions to within 10% automatically. At epoch 99: CE contribution 0.90, KL 1.00, AT 0.91 — despite the raw AT weight being 702× larger.

Tucker is faster despite more parameters

ResNet-34→18 CPU latency: 265.98 ms teacher → 135.88 ms CP (1.96×) → 125.91 ms Tucker (2.11×). Tucker carries 2.4× more parameters than CP yet runs faster because its 3-operation kernel chain issues fewer sequential CUDA/CPU kernel launches than CP's 4-operation chain, and its dense core convolution maps more efficiently to hardware.

Figure 3 — Accuracy–Compression Pareto Frontier (ResNet-34→ResNet-18, ImageNet-100) Each point is one (distillation method, decomposition) pair. Tucker points (squares) cluster at 6.56× compression; CP points (circles) at 16.04×. Tucker consistently dominates CP in accuracy at every compression level — the ordering never reverses. AT and FitNet are the top-performing objectives across both decompositions.
Figure 5 — EVBMF Per-Layer Rank Allocation (ResNet-34→ResNet-18, ImageNet-100) Top: CP ranks (absolute, 17 decomposable layers). Bottom: Tucker's separate (Rout, Rin) rank pairs. Both distributions are markedly non-uniform — early layers retain ≈59% of channels while deep layers fall to 36–44%. A uniform 50% ratio would over-allocate parameters to redundant deep layers and under-allocate them to the information-rich early layers.
Figure 7 — Training Convergence under AT Framework (ResNet-34→ResNet-18) Tucker (amber) leads CP (purple) at every single epoch — no crossover. After the first LR decay at epoch 30 Tucker reaches 80.12% and CP reaches 79.50%, a consistent gap of ≈0.62 pp through training. Teacher accuracy dashed at 81.72%.
Figure 8 — Kendall Uncertainty Loss-Weight Evolution (ResNet-34→ResNet-18) The three effective task weights exp(−s) for CE (blue), KL (red) and AT (green) on log scale over 100 epochs. The AT weight rises to ≈700× above CE, but this is scale normalisation, not dominance — at epoch 99 the weighted gradient contributions are CE 0.90, KL 1.00, AT 0.91: within 10% of each other.
Accuracy–Compression Pareto Frontier — ImageNet-100 (Both Pairs) Both ImageNet-100 pairs side-by-side. Left: ResNet-34→ResNet-18 (CP 16.0× / Tucker 6.6× smaller). Right: ResNet-50→ResNet-18 (CP 17.6× / Tucker 7.5× smaller). Tucker squares reach ~80.0–80.2% while CP circles trade ~2 pp for 2–3× extra parameter compression. FitNet and AT are the strongest distillation objectives across both pairs and both decompositions.
CP vs. Tucker per Distillation Method — ResNet-34→ResNet-18, ImageNet-100 Tucker (orange) outperforms CP (blue) under every distillation objective. The gap narrows with KD (77.2 vs. 77.3) and widens with FitNet (78.5 vs. 80.2) and AT (79.5 vs. 80.1). Teacher accuracy is 81.7% (dashed). Training Convergence — CP Student, ImageNet-100 Test Top-1 accuracy over 100 epochs for each distillation objective on both ImageNet-100 pairs. Training Convergence — Tucker Student, ImageNet-100 Same setup as above but for the Tucker student. Tucker curves track 1–2 pp above CP throughout, converging to 79.9–80.2% at epoch 100 under FitNet/AT. Training Convergence — CP Student, CIFAR-100 Six CIFAR-100 pairs trained for 250 epochs with cosine annealing. Easier same-family pairs (WRN-40-2→WRN-16-2) plateau near 75–76%; harder cross-architecture pairs (ResNet-110→ResNet-20) converge around 67–70%. Training Convergence — Tucker Student, CIFAR-100 Tucker counterpart to the CP convergence grid above. Curves are systematically higher and exhibit tighter method-to-method variance — Tucker's larger capacity stabilises the distillation signal across objectives. The rank ordering of methods is preserved (FitNet/AT on top), confirming that Tucker's advantage is structural: it holds regardless of which distillation loss is used.
Technologies Used

Built on a modern PyTorch research stack

The entire pipeline — from GPU-accelerated data loading to FLOP profiling — is reproducible end to end with a single shell script per dataset.

Language & Core Framework

Python 3.x PyTorch ≥ 2.0 DDP · AMP · torch.compile · torch.linalg torchvision transforms, dataset loaders

Distributed Training & Acceleration

NVIDIA DALI GPU-accelerated ImageNet-100 data pipeline NVIDIA Apex SyncBN + mixed precision for DDP DistributedDataParallel (torchrun) multi-GPU ImageNet training Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) GradScaler, float16 + float32

Numerics & Tensor Decomposition

TensorLy PARAFAC / Tucker decomposition (architecture init) SciPy ≥ 1.9.0 EVBMF numerical routines, CKA / CCA utilities NumPy ≥ 1.21.0 array operations, rank maps torch.linalg.eigh / svd per-layer EVBMF, Kendall weighting

Profiling, Monitoring & Utilities

THOP FLOPs + parameter counting on deployed models (CPU, no CUDA overhead) TensorBoard tensorboard-logger, training curves psutil RAM / memory monitoring during teacher caching tqdm epoch-level progress bars Kaggle API ImageNet-100 dataset acquisition Pillow ≥ 9.0.0 image I/O

Datasets

CIFAR-100 60k images · 32×32 · 100 classes · 50k train / 10k test · 240 epochs ImageNet-100 100-class subset of ILSVRC-2012 · 224×224 · 100 epochs · DALI pipeline

Architectures (CIFAR-100)

ResNet resnet8/14/20/32/44/56/110 · resnet8×4 · resnet32×4 WideResNet wrn_16_1 · wrn_16_2 · wrn_40_1 · wrn_40_2 VGG (BN) vgg8 · vgg11 · vgg13 · vgg16 · vgg19 MobileNetV2 full + mobile_half (half-width variant) ShuffleNet V1 / V2

Architectures (ImageNet-100)

ResNet-18 student (both pairs) ResNet-34 teacher (pair 1) ResNet-50 teacher (pair 2) ResNet-101 supported

Distillation Objectives Studied

KD Hinton et al. — logit-only KL divergence, temperature τ=4 FitNet Romero et al. — hint layer regression via ConvReg adapters AT Zagoruyko & Komodakis — spatial attention map MSE (framework default) VID Ahn et al. — variational information distillation, per-channel Gaussian NLL WSL Zhou et al. — weighted soft labels with focal weighting
